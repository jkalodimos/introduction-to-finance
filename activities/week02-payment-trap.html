Many cars depreciate 25–30% in year 1, in which case this buyer would already be underwater. The broader lesson: on a long-term loan, principal reduction is back-loaded while depreciation is front-loaded, so equity builds slowly and even moderate depreciation can leave the borrower underwater early in the loan.

Part IV — The key insight

Monthly payment is a function of four variables: price, down payment, rate, and term. The salesperson's real lever is term — stretching the loan out is what lets them hit a lower monthly payment. Rate isn't something they control; it's set by the market, and longer-term loans happen to carry a rate premium because they're riskier for the lender. The salesperson doesn't choose the higher rate, but they welcome the side-effect: it means more interest flowing to the lender over a longer period, while the customer's attention stays fixed on the monthly payment. Shopping by payment lets them move the one lever the customer isn't watching. Better questions: "What's the total cost of the loan?", "What's the shortest term that fits my budget?", "What's the rate, and does it change if I take a longer term?"
